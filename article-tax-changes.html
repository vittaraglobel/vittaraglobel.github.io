Key Tax Changes for 2025/26: What UK Individuals & Businesses Must Know

DF
Darshik Pratapbhai Fataniya
Founder & Director, Vittara
| 📅 1 April 2025 ⏱ 7 min read

The 2025/26 tax year brings a number of important changes that affect individuals, sole traders, and businesses across the UK. Whether you're filing a self-assessment return, running payroll, or planning your business finances, understanding these updates early gives you the best chance to minimise your tax liability and avoid costly penalties.

Important: Tax rules can change rapidly. While this article reflects our understanding as of April 2025, always consult a qualified accountant before making financial decisions based on tax planning. Contact Vittara for personalised advice.

Income Tax: Frozen Thresholds Continue

The personal allowance remains frozen at £12,570 until at least April 2028 — a policy that effectively increases the tax burden each year as wages rise with inflation. This "fiscal drag" means more of your income falls into higher tax bands without any nominal rate increase.

Tax BandRateIncome Range 2025/26
Personal Allowance0%Up to £12,570
Basic Rate20%£12,571 – £50,270
Higher Rate40%£50,271 – £125,140
Additional Rate45%Over £125,140

For higher earners, the personal allowance tapers to zero once income exceeds £100,000 — creating an effective 60% marginal rate between £100,000 and £125,140. This remains one of the most important tax planning opportunities for UK professionals.

National Insurance Contributions

The 2025/26 year sees changes to employer National Insurance that every business owner needs to plan for:

  • Employer NIC rate increases from 13.8% to 15% on earnings above the Secondary Threshold
  • The Secondary Threshold drops from £9,100 to £5,000, meaning employers pay NIC on a wider portion of wages
  • The Employment Allowance increases to £10,500, offering partial relief to smaller employers

Capital Gains Tax Rates

Following the October 2024 Autumn Budget, Capital Gains Tax rates have changed for disposals from 30 October 2024 onwards:

  • Basic rate taxpayers: CGT on most assets rises from 10% to 18%
  • Higher/additional rate taxpayers: CGT on most assets rises from 20% to 24%
  • Residential property rates remain at 18% and 24% (unchanged)
  • Business Asset Disposal Relief (BADR) — formerly Entrepreneurs' Relief — remains at 10% for the 2024/25 tax year but rises to 14% in 2025/26 and 18% from 2026/27

Inheritance Tax Changes

The nil-rate band remains at £325,000 and the residence nil-rate band at £175,000, frozen until 2030. However, significant changes are coming for agricultural and business property relief from April 2026, which will cap the relief at £1 million per person.

Additionally, from April 2027, pension death benefits will be brought within the scope of Inheritance Tax — a major shift that affects long-term retirement and estate planning.

VAT & Making Tax Digital

MTD for Income Tax Self Assessment (ITSA) launches in stages:

  • April 2026: Sole traders and landlords with income over £50,000 must comply
  • April 2027: Those with income over £30,000 must comply

This means quarterly digital reporting to HMRC will become mandatory for millions of self-employed individuals. Now is the time to ensure your bookkeeping software is MTD-compatible — Vittara can set up Xero or QuickBooks for you and handle the quarterly submissions.

Tax Planning Actions to Take Now

Given these changes, here are the priority actions for 2025/26:

  1. Review your salary/dividend split if you're a director-shareholder — the NIC changes may alter the optimal structure
  2. Use your ISA allowance (£20,000) and pension annual allowance (£60,000) before 5 April 2026
  3. Consider CGT crystallisation — if you have assets with significant gains, review whether realising gains before further rate increases makes sense
  4. Prepare for MTD — start using compliant software now rather than scrambling in 2026
  5. Review your estate plan given the IHT and pension changes announced for 2026/27
