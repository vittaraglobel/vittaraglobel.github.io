Retirement Planning:
Why Starting Early Changes Everything

📅 February 2025 ⏱ 7 min read ✍ Vittara Research Team

Most people know they should save for retirement. Far fewer appreciate just how dramatically the timing of those contributions affects the final outcome. The difference between starting at 25 and starting at 35 can be the difference between a comfortable retirement and a stressful one — even if the total amount contributed is identical.

In this guide, we break down how compound growth works, what the rules allow, and the practical steps UK individuals can take right now to build a stronger retirement pot.

The Power of Compound Growth

Compound growth is the single most powerful force in long-term wealth building. When your pension investments generate returns, those returns are reinvested — and then they earn returns themselves. Over decades, this creates an exponential curve that early savers benefit from and late starters can never fully replicate.

Same monthly contribution. Same growth rate. The 10-year head start nearly doubles the pot. That is the maths of compounding — and why starting now, even with a modest amount, beats waiting until you can afford to contribute more.

Understanding Your Pension Options

Workplace pension (auto-enrolment)

If you're employed and earn over £10,000 per year, you're automatically enrolled in a workplace pension. The minimum contribution is currently 8% of qualifying earnings (3% from your employer, 5% from you). This is the baseline — but the minimum is rarely enough on its own for a comfortable retirement.

Personal pension (SIPP)

A Self-Invested Personal Pension (SIPP) gives you more control over how your money is invested. You can hold a wide range of assets including funds, shares, and ETFs. SIPPs are ideal for the self-employed, contractors, and anyone who wants to top up their workplace pension.

Tax relief — the government contribution

One of the most powerful features of pension saving is tax relief. For every £80 you contribute as a basic-rate taxpayer, the government adds £20 — effectively boosting your contribution by 25%. Higher-rate taxpayers can reclaim an additional 20% through self-assessment, making each £60 net contribution worth £100 in the pension.

Director tip: If you run a limited company, employer pension contributions are a deductible business expense. Paying £10,000 per year into a director's pension can save up to £2,500 in corporation tax — money that would otherwise be taxed before it ever reached your pension.

How Much Is Enough?

The Pensions and Lifetime Savings Association (PLSA) provides useful benchmarks. A moderate retirement for a single person requires around £31,300 per year; a comfortable retirement around £43,100. The State Pension currently provides approximately £11,500 per year (full new State Pension), leaving a significant gap that personal savings must fill.

To produce £25,000 of income per year from your pension (to supplement the State Pension), you typically need a pot of approximately £500,000–£625,000 depending on how you draw it down.

A Practical Action Plan

  1. Consolidate old pensions

    The average person has 11 jobs during their career, potentially leaving behind 11 pension pots. Track them down via the government's Pension Tracing Service and consider consolidating them into a single SIPP for better oversight and lower fees.

  2. Increase employer contributions

    Check your workplace pension rules — many employers will match additional contributions above the minimum. Getting 3–5% from your employer costs you nothing except asking.

  3. Use Salary Sacrifice

    Salary sacrifice contributions reduce your gross salary, saving both Income Tax and National Insurance. For a 40% taxpayer, this can boost the effective value of each £1 contributed to as much as £1.67.

  4. Carry forward unused allowances

    Had a good year financially? You can contribute up to three prior years of unused annual allowance in one go — potentially up to £200,000 tax-efficiently in a single year.

  5. Review your investment strategy

    Most default pension funds are overly conservative for younger savers. Consider higher-equity allocations when retirement is 20+ years away, transitioning to bonds and lower-risk assets as you approach your target date.

The Death Benefit Change — Plan Ahead

From April 2027, unspent pension pots will be brought within the scope of Inheritance Tax — a major shift in estate planning. Pensions were previously one of the most efficient ways to pass wealth to the next generation. Now, families with large pension pots will need to revisit their estate planning strategy urgently.

If you are over 55 and have a significant pension, this change should prompt an immediate review of your overall estate and drawdown strategy.
